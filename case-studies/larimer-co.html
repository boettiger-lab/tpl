0What this walkthrough delivers

Imagine a hypothetical commission letter arrives in May 2026 from the chair of the Larimer County Board of Commissioners. Like Lake County's chair did in October 2023, they ask TPL Conservation Finance for “technical advice and assistance in connection with our efforts to develop and sustain reliable, ongoing sources of funding for land conservation, open space, and natural area protection and stewardship.” This is the standard TPL feasibility-study trigger.

The political context is loud. In November 2025, voters in Fort Collins (Larimer's largest city) approved a renewed $181 million sales tax for open space, watershed protection, trails, parks, and wildlife habitat at 73.1% yes — the largest local conservation win in Colorado in 2025. The county's own quarter-cent Help Preserve Open Spaces sales tax was renewed in 2014 at 81.67% yes and runs through 2043. Boulder County, next door, passed a $150M conservation sales tax in 2025 at 74.25% yes. The county BCC wants to know whether there is room — and demand — for a new countywide measure on the 2027 or 2028 ballot.

2§ Demographics (Lake Co. report § 3)

The Lake County report opens its Overview with a one-page demographics table sourced from US Census QuickFacts: population 2010 and 2020, percent change, race breakdown, owner-occupied housing rate, median home value, education attainment, household income, poverty rate, disability, and uninsured rate, all side-by-side with the Florida statewide figures.

Pull a US Census demographics table for Larimer County compared to Colorado statewide — population, race, housing, education, income, health.
The geo-agent does not currently hold US Census ACS or QuickFacts data. The closest available layer is CDC's Social Vulnerability Index 2022 at tract level (svi-2022), which does carry population, housing, income, language, and a half-dozen other ACS-derived variables. For a Lake-County-style demographics block, a future census-acs-county layer in the catalog would let the agent produce this section directly; in the meantime, the analyst pulls census.gov/quickfacts/larimercountycolorado and pastes the table verbatim.

3§ Existing conservation programs (Lake Co. report § 7)

This is the section the geo-agent shines on. The Lake County report's “Office of Parks and Trails / Lake County Water Authority / Florida Wildlife Corridor” sub-sections each ran to half a page of program description and acreage. The Larimer analog is:

  1. Larimer County Open Lands Program (county sales tax, since 1995)
  2. Fort Collins Natural Areas Department (city sales tax, since 1992)
  3. Great Outdoors Colorado (GOCO) (state lottery, since 1992)
  4. U.S. Forest Service / NPS / BLM (federal lands — most of the western county)

The Lake County feasibility study did not have a dedicated climate or biodiversity section. That's a function of when it was produced and the manual effort an analyst would have had to spend pulling carbon and species data from federal sources. With the geo-agent it takes two prompts.

The story this surfaces: of Larimer's roughly 6,840 km² (1.69M acres), about ~9,000 km² of footprint exists across all PAD-US protected units (overlapping counts because of layered designations). The federal portion dominates — Roosevelt NF alone covers ~3,240 km², or nearly half the western county, and Rocky Mountain NP's Larimer slice adds another ~1,113 km². How much irrecoverable carbon sits under the Conservation Almanac sites in Larimer County?
The H3 hex SEMI JOIN of Almanac sites × the irrecoverable-carbon-2024 hex parquet returns 0.001 Mt of irrecoverable carbon across 932 hex cells — a very small number relative to the volume in tropical, coastal, and Pacific-Northwest landscapes (Florida's protected lands carry 0.29 Mt; Oregon 0.5+ Mt under their voter-approved jurisdictions). Colorado's montane sagebrush, ponderosa, and short-grass prairie are not where the country's most irrecoverable-by-2050 carbon sits. This is worth knowing: a climate framing for the Larimer measure should lead with water (snowpack, the Cache la Poudre, wildfire watershed protection) rather than carbon sequestration.

The Colorado pattern is very different from Florida. In Florida, the bond mechanism dominates and passes at 88%. In Colorado, the sales tax dominates absolutely — 78% pass rate, $5.16B of the state's $7.3B in conservation dollars approved over 37 years. Property tax measures in CO are more volatile (only 64% pass) because of the way TABOR (Taxpayer's Bill of Rights, 1992) constrains mill-levy increases; sales tax measures are friendlier to TABOR rules and have established a generation of voter familiarity.

TABOR-specific legal context (de-Brucing, mill-levy cap, 30-year bonding limits, vote-of-the-people requirements for tax increases) is not in the geo-agent. The legal section of a Colorado feasibility study still gets cut-and-pasted from the state-team template and last verified with bond counsel.

The question for a 2027/28 ballot is therefore not “will voters support conservation funding,” but “does the additional ask sit comfortably alongside the existing $0.25 sales tax already authorized through 2043?”

Three things stand out for the Larimer brief:

  • Mechanism uniformity. Of 16 successful CO measures 2018–2025, 12 used sales tax. None used GO bonds. Property tax worked in Summit and Eagle Counties (small-population resort-economy counties); sales tax is the default everywhere else.
  • Margins are wide regardless of partisan lean. Douglas County (heavily Republican) cleared 87.6%; Adams County (Democratic) cleared 82.6%; Colorado Springs (Republican) cleared 78.0%; Boulder city (Democratic) cleared 85.8%. The Larimer 2014 county renewal at 81.7% sits squarely in this band.

A countywide Larimer measure in 2027–28 in this range would be neither unusually small nor unusually large.

8§ Public Finance Options for Larimer (Lake Co. report § 8)

The Lake County study laid out, in turn, GO bonds, bonds with millage, ballot-language requirements, and enactment procedures, with a precedent table. In Colorado the operative menu is different.

What the data say about mechanism choice

From § 5 above: sales tax is the dominant mechanism in Colorado (78% pass rate, $5.16B of the state's conservation dollars). Property tax (mill-levy increases) pass less reliably (64%) and are constrained by TABOR. GO bonds have been used for school and infrastructure but are rare for conservation.

What's missing from the geo-agent

The Lake County report's strongest single page is the bond-cost table — “$50M bond @ 5% / 20-year = $4M annual debt service = $21/year per median-value homeowner.” Producing that table requires (a) the county's total taxable value and median home value (from the county assessor), (b) current municipal interest rate estimates (from a financial advisor or bond counsel), and (c) a simple amortization calculator. None of those three are in the geo-agent. This section of any feasibility study will continue to require an external bond-counsel or financial-advisor partner.

What the geo-agent gives you cleanly

The agent can identify at what historic dollar size a Colorado sales-tax measure has been winning and against what opposition. Sales-tax measures in the $50M–$200M range, with a 20-year sunset and a clear conservation purpose (open space, water quality, trails, wildlife habitat), have a track record of clearing 70%+ approval in both red and blue Colorado counties.

Larimer County, Colorado — Public Finance Feasibility Study · Draft Executive Summary · May 2026

The case for a 2027/2028 conservation ballot measure

Larimer County has the strongest local-conservation-finance record in Colorado outside the immediate Denver metro: 11 measures on the ballot since 1992, 9 passed, all using sales tax, totaling $601 million authorized for open space, parks, trails, and wildlife habitat. The 2014 countywide renewal of Help Preserve Open Spaces cleared 81.7% yes and runs through 2043. In 2025 Fort Collins voters approved a new $181M city sales-tax measure at 73.1% yes — the largest city-level conservation win in Colorado that cycle. Voter support is not the question.

Existing investment, recorded in the TPL Conservation Almanac

  • 227 sites · 99,836 acres · $190.4M of tracked funding, 1994–2018.
  • Lead programs: Larimer Open Lands + Fort Collins Natural Areas ($128M local); Great Outdoors Colorado ($33M state lottery); LWCF + Forest Legacy ($9M federal).
  • Iconic acquisitions: Red Mountain Open Space (13,448 ac), Soapstone Prairie (16,452 ac across phases), Roberts Ranch easements (16,528 ac), Hermit Park (1,362 ac), Devil's Backbone (1,764 ac).

Protected-land context

  • Roosevelt National Forest covers ~3,240 km² of the western county; Rocky Mountain NP adds another ~1,113 km². New county/city measures operate primarily in the foothills and plains of the east.
  • Fort Collins Natural Areas: 339 PAD-US units, ~683 km². County Open Lands: 72 units, ~271 km². Land trusts (TNC, Audubon, Foothills): 217 units, ~363 km².

Colorado statewide context

  • 263 conservation measures since 1988, 73% pass rate, $7.3B approved.
  • Sales tax accounts for 118 measures, 78% pass rate, $5.16B — dominant mechanism.
  • Property tax measures pass at 64% (TABOR friction). GO bonds rare in CO conservation.

Recent peer-county comparables

  • Boulder County 2025 — $150M sales tax @ 74.2% yes
  • Colorado Springs 2023 — $144M sales tax @ 78.0% yes (Republican-leaning)
  • Douglas County 2022 — $108.8M sales tax @ 87.6% yes (Republican-leaning)
  • Arapahoe County 2021 — $112M sales tax @ 76.7% yes
  • Adams County 2020 — $200M sales tax @ 82.6% yes

What the geo-agent says about a 2027/28 measure

  • The political record supports a sales-tax measure in the $100–200M range.
  • The existing county $0.25 Help Preserve Open Spaces sales tax runs to 2043, so a new ballot question would be additive — most plausibly framed as a "new initiative" (climate, water, fire-resilience, equity) rather than as a renewal.
  • Equity: five of Larimer's 79 census tracts sit at SVI ≥ 0.75 — eastern Fort Collins, parts of Loveland, the Estes Park area — and should be the explicit benefit focus of any new ask.
  • Climate framing: Larimer's irrecoverable-carbon load is low compared to coastal or PNW peers; a stronger frame is water (Cache la Poudre watershed, post–Cameron Peak Fire recovery, snowpack reliability) and biodiversity (Laramie Foothills wildlife migration corridor).

11Methodology & reproducibility

Every figure in this case study traces to one of three datasets: landvote (3,886 ballot measures, 1988–2025), conservation-almanac-2024-sites + -funding (67,428 protected sites with full funding history), and the Census 2024 county boundary parquet (FIPS 08069 = Larimer). Spatial intersections use H3 hex resolution-8 SEMI JOINs as documented in the geo-agent's H3 guide; each query above can be pasted into the duckdb-mcp tool directly.

The PAD-US section uses pad-us-4.1-combined; the SVI tract data use svi-2022. The irrecoverable-carbon overlay uses irrecoverable-carbon-2024. Almanac coverage for Colorado ends in 2018 — any 2019–2026 acquisitions must come from the Larimer Open Lands annual report and Fort Collins Natural Areas tracking.

Total time to produce this walkthrough's app-driven sections: approximately 90 minutes of prompting on the geo-agent app. Total time the team has saved versus pulling the same content manually from LandVote.org's web interface plus state-by-state DEP records: approximately 6–8 hours.

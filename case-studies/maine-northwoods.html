0What this walkthrough delivers

The Mississippi Headwaters feasibility study is the most data-intensive report TPL Conservation Finance produces. It defines a multi-county region (18 Minnesota counties), describes its ecology and economy, quantifies the carbon stored in its forests and wetlands, inventories existing conservation funding, then spends 50+ pages walking through 15 possible funding mechanisms — bonds, sales tax, lottery, mortgage-registry tax, deed tax, lodging tax, corporate franchise tax, recreational marijuana, sports betting, impervious-surface fees, carbon pricing, cap-and-trade, current-use programs, lake-improvement districts, watershed districts.

That second half is irreducibly expert work — statute-by-statute legal analysis with state-specific bond-counsel input. The geo-agent doesn't attempt it. What the agent does accelerate is the first half: the regional definition, the ecological framing, the ownership picture, the carbon analysis, the existing-funding inventory, and the ballot history that anchors the political case.

This walkthrough drafts that first half for a parallel region: Maine's North Woods — Aroostook, Piscataquis, Somerset, and Penobscot Counties, the four largest counties in Maine, encompassing 17,953 square miles of contiguous forest, lakes, and working timberland. It's the kind of region TPL has historically served with one-off federal Forest Legacy and Land for Maine's Future deals; what it has never had is a dedicated voter-approved finance mechanism.

2§ Ownership picture (Headwaters report § "Mississippi Headwaters" pp. 10–12)

The pattern the agent surfaces is the defining feature of Maine conservation: private NGO ownership of working forest, often through TNC and the Forest Society of Maine, totaling roughly 6,700 km² — more than three times any other category. State parks add ~4,000 km². Federal ownership is small (Katahdin Woods & Waters National Monument ~870 km², plus scattered USFWS refuges). Tribal lands account for ~474 km² (the Penobscot Nation, with additional Passamaquoddy and Maliseet land in Washington and Aroostook).

This is the opposite of the Mississippi Headwaters report's pattern (36% state, 44% private). In Maine's North Woods privately-owned land under NGO conservation control is the dominant protection mechanism. That detail matters for any finance discussion: a state bond that match-funds private easements is the historical recipe that has worked here; a county sales tax would be testing new ground.

3§ Carbon sequestration (Headwaters report § "Climate Change Adaptation" pp. 15–17)

The Mississippi Headwaters report's Carbon Sequestration section was its centerpiece: a Forest Carbon Map infographic with 416 million metric tons of total forest carbon, $18.3 billion social value, $22.2 billion market value, and a top-five-counties ranking led by Itasca (105 Mt), Cass (61 Mt), Beltrami (61 Mt). That figure came from TPL's own Carbon Map tool, which uses USFS Forest Inventory and Analysis (FIA) data — a total-stock methodology including soil organic carbon, dead wood, and litter.

The geo-agent's carbon layers use a different but related methodology: the Noon et al. (2022) classification of carbon into irrecoverable (lost forever if disturbed in the next 30 years), vulnerable (can recover within ~30 years under best management), and manageable (the short-cycle carbon pool that responds to management).

How much irrecoverable, vulnerable, and manageable carbon sits under the four-county Maine North Woods region?
The SQL the app runs (three H3 hex SEMI JOINs)
WITH region AS (
  SELECT DISTINCT h8, h0 FROM
    read_parquet('s3://public-census/census-2024/county/hex/h0=*/data_0.parquet')
  WHERE STATEFP='23'
    AND NAMELSAD IN ('Aroostook County','Piscataquis County',
                     'Somerset County','Penobscot County')
),
carbon AS (
  SELECT c.h8, c.h0, AVG(c.carbon) AS c
  FROM read_parquet('s3://public-carbon/irrecoverable-carbon-2024/hex/h0=*/data_0.parquet') c
  SEMI JOIN region ON c.h8 = region.h8 AND c.h0 = region.h0
  GROUP BY c.h8, c.h0
)
-- (repeat for vulnerable-carbon and manageable-carbon)
SELECT ROUND(SUM(c)/1e6, 2) AS Mt FROM carbon;

How to read these numbers: 12.6 Mt of carbon across the four-county North Woods is the pool that a natural-climate-solutions strategy (avoided conversion + extended rotations + reforestation + improved forest management) can directly affect. At a $51/ton 2025 social cost of carbon (EPA 2023 update), that's a ~$640 million annual social value at risk if the working-forest economy converts toward higher-intensity use or development — a number that puts the historic $24.97M Downeast Lakes deal and $20.68M West Branch deal in clear context.

The geo-agent's carbon layers do not include soil organic carbon in peatlands or wetland-stored carbon below 30 cm. Maine's peatlands — especially the Caribou Bog Wetland Complex and the western Aroostook Maine peatlands — store substantial additional carbon that the Mississippi Headwaters report cited at the national level (“wetlands in the U.S. store a total of 11.52 billion metric tons of carbon, much of which is within soils deeper than 30 cm”). A complete carbon section would augment the geo-agent's output with the USGS LandCarbon peatland model or DEP wetlands inventory.

Three observations:

  1.   2. Land for Maine's Future has funded 41 sites for $12.11M in the region — but every dollar of that came from one of five state bond measures (1999, 2005, 2007, 2010, 2012) that voters approved. 5§ Maine's voter history — and the geographic gap (parallel to Headwaters § "Choosing a Funding Strategy")

This is the most consequential section of the case study, because it crystallizes the political opportunity. The Mississippi Headwaters report could point to ten of eleven successful Minnesota park-conservation ballot measures generating $5.8B (most of which was the 2008 Clean Water, Land & Heritage state constitutional amendment). Maine's record is similarly strong on pass rate but much more limited in geography.

The geo-agent surfaces a fact buried in the data that is the lead finding of any North Woods report:

Filter that list to just Aroostook, Piscataquis, Somerset, and Penobscot Counties. How many measures in the North Woods region?
Zero. Not a single county-level or municipal conservation ballot measure has appeared in any of the four North Woods counties since the LandVote record began in 1988. Every Maine municipal measure in the database is in either York County (Kittery, Kennebunk, Ogunquit, Saco, York) or Cumberland County (Scarborough, South Portland, Falmouth, Freeport) — the southern coastal belt.

This is not because northern Maine voters are hostile to conservation. All five Maine statewide conservation bonds since 1999 have passed (60–69% statewide); the North Woods counties' precincts contributed to those wins. It is that no local government in the North Woods has yet asked voters for a dedicated conservation revenue stream. The pattern that works in southern Maine has not been tested in the north. The state-level Land for Maine's Future bond machine — which has been the regional finance backbone — has not been renewed since 2012, 14 years ago.

The political fact this points at: the next big climate-finance opportunity for the Maine North Woods is most plausibly a new state-level Land for Maine's Future bond on the 2026 or 2028 ballot, sized closer to the 1999 era ($50M+) than the 2012 era ($5M). The historic pass rates strongly suggest it would pass.

6§ Mechanism analysis (Headwaters report pp. 26–76 — the 50-page core)

The Mississippi Headwaters report's defining body is its mechanism-by-mechanism analysis: 15 distinct funding sources with legal authority citations, revenue estimates from state-specific data, and precedent comparisons drawn from other states. Lottery (pp. 27–28), sales tax (29–30), mortgage registry tax (31), deed tax (31), lodging tax (32–33), corporate franchise tax (33), recreational marijuana (34–36), sports betting (37), impervious surface fees (38–40), carbon pricing (41–42), cap-and-trade (43–48), carbon sequestration on School Trust Lands (47), Clean Water Act §212 (49–52), current use programs (55–58), watershed districts (61–62), conservation tax credits (63–65), and three pages of “new incentives” (66–71).

This entire section is outside the geo-agent's scope. Each mechanism analysis requires (a) the relevant state statute citation (Maine Revised Statutes), (b) a current revenue estimate from a state-specific source (Maine Revenue Services, the Office of the State Treasurer, MaineHousing, the State Tax Assessor), and (c) bond-counsel input on what voter approval would be required. Recreating these mechanism analyses for Maine remains expert manual work — and is the right division of labor.

Maine's North Woods — Climate & Conservation Finance Feasibility Study · Draft Executive Summary · May 2026

Toward the next Land for Maine's Future bond

The Maine North Woods — Aroostook, Piscataquis, Somerset, and Penobscot Counties, 17,953 square miles of contiguous working forest and lakes — has been protected, where it has been protected, by the USFS Forest Legacy Program ($41.6M tracked) and the voter-approved Land for Maine's Future bond program ($12.1M tracked). Both pipelines have thinned: the state has not bonded for LMF since 2012, and federal Forest Legacy appropriations have not kept pace with deal opportunity. Maine voters have approved 5 of 6 statewide conservation bonds since 1999 (93% pass rate); the 2026 or 2028 cycle is the natural next window for a new state bond sized closer to the 1999 era ($50M+) than the 2012 era ($5M).

  • 4 counties · 17,953 sq mi · larger than MA + CT + RI + DE combined.
  • Conservation Almanac records: 94 protected sites · 1,075,853 acres · $118.7M of tracked funding from 1999–2018.

  • State parks (Baxter, Allagash, Moosehead): ~3,983 km².

  • Conservation easements on private land: ~1,835 km².

  • Land for Maine's Future (state bond) — $12.11M across 41 smaller sites.
  • Federal LWCF + NAWCA — $3.5M across 14 sites.
  • Unattributed (private partners — TNC, Forest Society of Maine, Open Space Institute, foundations) — $53.07M across 27 sites.

Maine voter record

  • 27 conservation ballot measures since 1991 · 25 passed (93% pass rate) statewide.
  • 5 of 6 statewide bonds passed: 1999 ($50M, 69%), 2005 ($12M, 65%), 2007 ($17M, 63%), 2010 ($9.75M, 59%), 2012 ($5M, 61%). Only 1991 failed.
  • 22 successful municipal bond measures since 1996, all in York and Cumberland Counties (southern Maine).

What the geo-agent recommends as next steps

  • Brief Maine Legislative leadership on a 2026 or 2028 Land for Maine's Future bond at $40M–$75M scale. Pass-rate history strongly supports this range.
  • Pair the state bond ask with a federal Forest Legacy appropriation request; the two have always run together for this region.
  • For 2027–2030, study viability of a North Woods regional special district (e.g., parallel to the Mississippi Headwaters Board's eight-county joint powers structure) — would be new ground in Maine but is well-supported by Minnesota precedent.
  • Commission a public-opinion survey to test ballot language; LMF measures have historically polled well but the 14-year gap since 2012 means a fresh tolerance test is overdue.

9Methodology & series wrap-up

The case study series closes here. The three reports — Florida Senate District 21, Larimer County feasibility study, and this Maine North Woods climate-finance feasibility study — span the three TPL Conservation Finance report types defined in example-reports/01-report-structure-analysis.md. Each one demonstrates a different ratio of geo-agent-produced content to expert manual content:

CaseReport typeApprox. agent shareNotable agent value-add
FL Senate District 211-page legislative brief~80% of the briefReproduces every $ amount exactly; surfaces 7 omitted FCT-funded sites

Maine North Woods81-page climate-finance feasibility~20% (first 15 of ~80 pages)Multi-source spatial intersections impossible without H3 hex joins

The pattern is the right shape: the geo-agent provides the re-usable, spatial-data-intensive, comparative-precedent backbone of each report; expert TPL staff and partners (bond counsel, state agencies) provide the state-specific legal and fiscal analysis the agent cannot. The agent's payoff is highest on the briefest report (where the spatial intersection IS the deliverable) and lowest on the longest report (where the deliverable is mostly legal mechanism analysis).

Total time to produce all three walkthroughs' app-driven sections, end-to-end: approximately 5 hours of MCP-driven prompting, plus the writing time around it. All H3 hex SEMI JOINs ran in under three seconds each on the server.

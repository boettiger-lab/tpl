# TPL Report Structure Analysis

## Overview

TPL's Conservation Finance Department produces three distinct report types, each serving a different stage of the advocacy and funding pipeline. All share a common thread: assembling geographic, demographic, fiscal, electoral, and conservation data for a specific jurisdiction to build the case for public conservation funding.

---

## Report Type 1: Legislative District Briefing

**Example:** *Florida Senate District 21 -- Florida Forever Projects* (1 page)

### Purpose
A concise advocacy document for a specific legislator summarizing conservation investments and voter support within their district. Designed to be handed to a legislator or staffer to justify a funding request.

### Structure
1. **Header:** District number, legislator name, party, and city
2. **Legislative request:** A specific dollar ask tied to a named program (e.g., "Dedicate a minimum of $300 million from the Land Acquisition Trust Fund to the Florida Forever Trust Fund")
3. **Project highlights by program:** Named conservation sites within the district, each with acreage and dollar amount. Grouped by funding program (e.g., Florida Communities Trust, Division of Recreation and Parks). Examples:
   - Pasco Palms, Pasco County: 116 acres, $360,000
   - Wall Springs, Pinellas County: 128 acres, $11.4 million
   - Werner-Boyce Salt Springs State Park: 78 acres, $2.4 million
4. **District-level totals:** Aggregate acres and dollars over a time period (1,149 acres; $27.98 million from 1998-2009)
5. **Voter support data:** Ballot measure results within the district (73% Yes on Amendment 1, 2014; 125,089 Yes votes)
6. **Contact info:** TPL staff name, title, phone, email
7. **Photo:** A local conservation site image

### Data Requirements
- Conservation site names, acreage, funding amounts within a legislative district
- Funding program attribution per site
- Ballot measure results at the district level (vote counts and percentages)
- Aggregate conservation spending totals over time for the district

## Report Type 2: Local Public Finance Feasibility Study

**Example:** *Lake County, Florida -- Public Finance Feasibility Study* (23 pages, December 2023)

### Purpose
A comprehensive analysis commissioned by a local government (county or municipality) evaluating whether and how to pursue dedicated public funding for conservation. Provides an objective, fact-based reference for county commissioners or staff to evaluate financing mechanisms.

### Structure

#### 1. Introduction (pp. 4-5)
- TPL organizational background and track record (e.g., "involved in more than 650 successful ballot measures," "$94.2 billion in new funding")
- State-specific ballot measure history (e.g., "41 of 48 measures in Florida" approved since 1996)
- Scope of the engagement and disclaimers

#### 2. Executive Summary (p. 6)
- Recommended funding mechanism (general obligation bond + property tax)
- Key findings in bullet form:
  - Bond cost examples ($50M bond = ~$4M annual debt service = $21/year per homeowner)
  - Advantages of debt instruments over pay-as-you-go
  - State-specific legal authority

#### 3. Overview: Demographics (p. 7)
A detailed demographics table comparing the jurisdiction to the state, sourced from US Census QuickFacts:
- Population (2010, 2020, percent change): Lake County 383,956 vs. Florida 21.5M
- Race/ethnicity breakdown (White 82%, Black 12.4%, Hispanic 18.7%)
- Housing (75.3% owner-occupied, median home value $223,000)
- Education (90.4% high school, 25.3% bachelor's)
- Income (median household $60,013, 10% poverty)
- Health (10.3% disability, 14.6% uninsured)

#### 4. Governance (p. 8)
- Governing body structure (5-member Board of County Commissioners)
- Commissioner names, districts, term expiration dates
- Election cycle details (3 seats up in 2024)

#### 5. Finances (pp. 8-9)
- Total governmental revenues ($415.4M in 2022) and expenditures ($394.9M)
- Revenue source breakdown (property tax, sales tax, gas tax, tourist development tax)
- Expenditure categories with percentages (public safety 49%, general government 26%)
- **Sales tax** details: 1% discretionary infrastructure tax, renewed 2015, extended through 2032
- **Property tax** details: millage rates by taxing district (General 5.0364 mills, Stormwater/Parks/Roads MSTU 0.4957 mills), comparison to rollback rates
- **Debt** position: $96M total bonded debt ($9.5M GO bonds, $86.4M revenue bonds), credit rating (Fitch AA), maturity schedule table

#### 6. Existing Conservation Programs (pp. 10-11)
- **Office of Parks and Trails:** 2004 referendum ($36M bonds, 71% approval), ~3,000 acres acquired, evaluation criteria
- **Lake County Water Authority:** 7,500 acres acquired, $8.3M annual revenue, NuRF facility, stormwater matching funds (~$10M)
- **Florida Wildlife Corridor:** 18M acres statewide, 112,651 "Opportunity Area" acres in county, $2B legislative budget since 2021

#### 7. Choosing a Local Funding Strategy (pp. 11-12)
- Three primary revenue types: discretionary spending, dedicated funding streams, debt financing
- Advantages of ballot measures over budget appropriations
- State enabling authority overview

#### 8. Public Finance Options (pp. 12-15)
- **History table:** Local conservation finance measures in Florida 1996-2020 (79 passed of 96 total, 82% approval; Bonds 88%, Sales Tax 64%, Property Tax 85%)
- **General Obligation Bonds:** Legal authority, duration limits (40 years), bond cost table:

  | Bond Issue | Rate | Maturity | Annual Debt Service | Annual Cost/Median Home | Monthly Cost |
  |---|---|---|---|---|---|
  | $50M | 5% | 20yr | $4,012,129 | $21 | $1.78 |
  | $100M | 5% | 20yr | $8,024,259 | $43 | $3.56 |
  | $150M | 5% | 20yr | $12,036,388 | $64 | $5.34 |

- **Bonds + Additional Millage:** Combined bond + operations funding via F.S. 200.18; revenue projections at 0.2-0.3 mill levies ($6.9M-$10.4M annually)
- **Ballot language requirements:** 75-word summary, 15-word title, single-subject rule
- **Precedent examples:** Manatee County 2020 ($50M, 71% approval), Flagler County 2008 ($40M, 65%)
- **Enactment procedures:** Referendum requirements, notice periods, election timing

#### 9. Elections (pp. 16-18)
- Election calendar with deadlines for 2024 cycle
- **Voter turnout history table:** Nov-22 (58%), Nov-20 (80%), Mar-20 (31%)
- Past ballot measure results relevant to fiscal questions (2004 land bond 71%, 2014 Amendment 1 70%, 2022 school safety levy 56% at $187/year)
- Voting methods and technology details

#### 10. Appendices (pp. 19-21)
- **Appendix A:** Property acquisitions catalog with individual sites (Dead River 8.6 acres/$1.165M, Ellis Acres 411 acres/$6.553M, etc.), property partnerships, property grants
- **Appendix B:** Map showing corridor opportunity areas, conserved lands, trail proximity analysis, public access lands
- **Appendix C:** Florida Wildlife Corridor opportunity area map for the county

### Data Requirements
- US Census demographics (ACS QuickFacts)
- County financial statements (CAFR/ACFR): revenues, expenditures, debt schedules
- Property appraiser data: total taxable value, median home values, millage rates
- Supervisor of Elections data: voter turnout, registration, election calendar
- Conservation site inventories with acreage and purchase prices
- State-specific ballot measure history (from LandVote database)
- Wildlife corridor / ecological network data
- Water authority financial data and land holdings
- State enabling statutes for bonding and taxation

## Report Type 3: Regional Climate & Conservation Finance Feasibility Study

**Example:** *Mississippi Headwaters -- Climate and Conservation Finance Feasibility Study* (81 pages, October 2019)

### Purpose
A comprehensive analysis combining climate science, natural climate solutions (NCS), and conservation finance to identify funding mechanisms for a multi-county watershed region. Commissioned by a state legislative committee (Senator Carrie Ruud, MN Senate District 10).

### Structure

#### 1. Introduction & Executive Summary (pp. 7-9)
- TPL track record in the state (158 project closings, ~100,000 acres in Minnesota; 10 of 11 successful ballot measures generating $5.8B)
- Climate framing: U.S. forests remove 12-14% of GHG emissions; wetlands store 11.52B metric tons of carbon nationally
- Summary of funding recommendations: GO bonds, lottery, sales tax, corporate franchise tax, deed tax, carbon pricing, cap and trade, marijuana tax, sports betting, and more

#### 2. The Mississippi Headwaters (pp. 10-14)
- **Geographic definition:** 18 counties, 1,255,105 acres, Upper Mississippi River Basin (20,100 sq mi)
- **Ecological significance:** 350+ species, 25% of North American fish species, 466 miles of protected river
- **Economic significance:** $400B+ annual economic impact, 1M jobs, $21.4B recreation economy, 351,000 tourism jobs
- **Land ownership breakdown:** 44% private, 36.1% state, 18% federal, 0.4% county, 0.8% Leech Lake Band of Ojibwe
- **Threats with charts:**
  - Nitrogen loads chart (1970-2040): +75% by 1990s, +40% by 2010s, +10.2% projected
  - Land conversion: 100,000 acres to development (2007-2012), 250,000 acres to cropland (2008-2012)
  - Developed land vs. population growth chart (1980-2020)

#### 3. Climate Change Adaptation, Resilience, and Mitigation (pp. 15-18)
- **Carbon Sequestration section with Forest Carbon Map infographic:**
  - Total Forest Carbon: 416,584,383 metric tons
  - Social Value: $18.3B; Market Value: $22.2B
  - Top 5 counties ranked by carbon stock (Itasca: 105M mt, Cass: 61M, Beltrami: 61M, Aitkin: 59M, Hubbard: 27M)
  - Equivalency metrics (172B gallons gasoline, 165M homes' energy use)
- **NCS Opportunities:** Avoided conversion, forest management, agricultural management
- Bar chart comparing Climate Alliance States' forest/urban tree carbon offset percentages

#### 4. Current Conservation Funding (p. 18)
- Overview of existing state programs and how to leverage them for climate mitigation

#### 5. Fiscal Health, Budget and Debt (pp. 18-20)
- State economy overview (2.8% unemployment, AAA credit rating, $1.544B surplus)
- **Debt capacity tables:** Three Capital Investment Guidelines with specific dollar thresholds ($2.738B under Guideline 1, $7.090B under Guideline 2)
- **Maximum Annual New Debt Authorizations table** (FY2019-2024 projections)
- **State budget pie charts:** Revenue sources (Individual Income 54.4%, Sales Tax 24.9%, Corporate 5.5%) and expenditure breakdown (Education 41.3%, Health/Human Services 31.4%)

#### 6. Tourism, Outdoor Recreation Economy (p. 23)
- Detailed recreation statistics: 70% participation rate, $16.7B annual spending, 140,000 jobs, $1.4B tax revenue
- Hunting: 477,000 hunters, $733M retail, 5.6M days
- Fishing/wildlife: 1.7M anglers, $2.4B spending

#### 7. Best Practices from Other States (pp. 24-25)
- Case studies: California ($4.1B bond, 58%), Colorado lottery ($63.7M/yr), Oregon Measure 76, Nebraska trust ($280M over 24 years), Ohio Clean Ohio Fund ($800M)

#### 8. Choosing a Funding Strategy -- Mechanism-by-Mechanism Analysis (pp. 26-76)
Each mechanism gets its own section with: description, legal authority, revenue estimates, precedents from other states, and applicability assessment. Mechanisms analyzed:
- **Lottery** (pp. 27-28): Current $38M allocation; constitutional amendment history (1988: 81%, 1990: 81%, 1998: 77%)
- **Sales tax** (pp. 29-30): ~$80M from sporting goods; $800M total sports goods sales
- **Mortgage Registry Tax** (pp. 31): $122M FY18; 0.07% increase = $37M/yr
- **Deed Tax** (pp. 31): $128M FY18; 0.07% increase = $27M/yr
- **Lodging Tax** (pp. 32-33): ~120 jurisdictions generating ~$77M
- **Corporate Franchise Tax** (pp. 33): 9.8% rate, $1.31B revenue; 0.5% increase = $67M
- **Recreational Marijuana** (pp. 34-36): Michigan $134M, California $214M, Colorado $266M projections
- **Sports Betting** (pp. 37): Delaware model; potential $20M/yr in MN
- **Impervious Surface Fees** (pp. 38-40): 198 utilities in MN; avg $5.34/month
- **Carbon Pricing** (pp. 41-42): $30/ton potential; Washington I-1631 case study ($2.3B projected)
- **Cap and Trade** (pp. 43-48): RGGI ($2.7B generated), California AB 32 ($11.8B auction revenue), Massachusetts ($470M)
- **Carbon Sequestration on School Trust Lands** (pp. 47): 31,637 acres; revenue scenarios from $100K to $1M+
- **Clean Water Act Section 212** (pp. 49-52): $133B nationally; $266M in MN (1987-2018)
- **Current Use programs** (pp. 55-58): Green Acres, Rural Preserve, Sustainable Forest Incentive Act
- **Lake Improvement Districts** (pp. 59-60)
- **Watershed Districts** (pp. 61-62)
- **Conservation Tax Credits** (pp. 63-65)
- **New Incentives** (pp. 66-71): Public awareness, procurement, building codes, tax incentives
- **Local Financing Authority** (pp. 72-76): GO bonds, deed recording fees, local options

#### 9. Next Steps (p. 77)
- Narrow to best-fit mechanisms
- Test voter attitudes via public opinion survey
- Align with state planning processes

#### 10. Appendix: Current State Programs (pp. 78-81)
- MN DNR funding sources: General Operating Fund, Game and Fish Fund, Natural Resources Fund
- Constitutionally-dedicated funds: Environment & Natural Resources Trust Fund, Outdoor Heritage Fund, Clean Water Fund, Parks and Trails Fund
- Reinvest in Minnesota program, Metropolitan Council

### Data Requirements
- Forest carbon stock data by county (metric tons, social value, market value)
- Carbon equivalency calculators (EPA GHG Equivalencies)
- Land use/land cover change data (NLCD or similar)
- Water quality data (nitrogen loads, pollutant trends)
- State budget documents (biennial budget, debt capacity forecasts)
- State-level tax revenue data (lottery, sales, deed, mortgage, corporate)
- Recreation and tourism economic statistics
- Multi-state comparative data on funding mechanisms
- Carbon market pricing data and program outcomes (RGGI, CA cap-and-trade)
- Federal program funding data (Clean Water Act, LWCF)
- State conservation program inventories and budgets

## Generic Instructions: How to Prepare a TPL Feasibility Report

If you were a new TPL Conservation Finance analyst preparing one of these reports, here is what you would need to do:

### For a Legislative District Briefing:
1. **Identify the district and legislator** (name, party, city, district number)
2. **Query the Conservation Almanac** for all conservation sites within the district boundaries -- extract site names, acreage, dollar amounts, and funding programs
3. **Aggregate totals** for the district over the relevant time period
4. **Pull LandVote data** for ballot measure results within the district (percentage yes, vote counts)
5. **Identify the current legislative ask** (specific dollar amount and program name from the state advocacy team)
6. **Format as a one-page brief** with a local photo

### For a Local Feasibility Study:
1. **Gather demographics** from US Census QuickFacts (population, race/ethnicity, housing, income, education, health)
2. **Research governance** (governing body composition, election cycles, commissioner names/terms)
3. **Analyze county finances** from the CAFR: revenues, expenditures, debt schedules, millage rates, taxable value
4. **Inventory existing conservation programs** in the jurisdiction (past referenda, land acquisition programs, water authorities, special districts)
5. **Research state-specific legal authority** for bonding and taxation mechanisms
6. **Calculate bond cost estimates** using total taxable value, assumed interest rates, and maturity periods
7. **Compile election history** (turnout, past fiscal ballot measures, approval percentages)
8. **Research precedent measures** in neighboring jurisdictions
9. **Identify ecological priorities** (wildlife corridors, water resources, environmentally sensitive lands)
10. **Produce maps** showing conserved lands, opportunity areas, and trail/park access

### For a Regional Climate & Finance Feasibility Study:
All of the above, plus:
1. **Compile carbon data** by county/watershed (forest carbon stocks, sequestration rates, social/market values)
2. **Gather land use change data** showing conversion trends (forest/wetland/grassland to development/agriculture)
3. **Analyze water quality trends** (nutrient loading, pollutant data)
4. **Research 15+ funding mechanisms** with revenue estimates, legal authority, and precedents from other states
5. **Compile state budget data** (revenue sources, expenditure categories, debt capacity forecasts)
6. **Gather outdoor recreation economy statistics** (participation rates, spending, jobs, tax revenue)
7. **Research carbon market programs** (RGGI, California cap-and-trade, voluntary markets) with dollar outcomes
8. **Document existing state conservation programs** with funding levels and statutory authority
